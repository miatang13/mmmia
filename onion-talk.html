User Research & UX Design | CMU Design Fall 2019

Duration: 4 Weeks
Skills: User Research Methods, Human-Centered Design, UI / UX Design
Collaborators: Youie Cho, Charmaine Qiu, Sarah Lee

My Role: User Research, User Interface Design, Motion Design

OnionTalk is a month-long, user research intensive, collaborative project aimed to enhance students' experience at CMU, focusing on the particular process of course selection.

Part A /
User Research

Traditional Research Methods:
Digital Survey & In-Person Interviews
Creative Methods:
Digital Web Experiment & Physical Card Game

Traditional Research Methods: Digital Survey

In total we received 50 reponses for our digital survey. We affirmed the fact that students are not very satisfied with the course selecting and dropping process, with the observation that students mainly dropped out due to the unmanageable workload. Even though our team thought that the main reason for the students’ dissatisfaction were due to misinformation on the syllabus, the survey concluded that syllabi are in fact very accurate, and that students did not know about the courses enough before registering for them.

Traditional Research Methods: In-Person Interview

For the in-person interviews, the main purpose was to specifically ask and talk about students’ past experience with dropping courses and to better understand their standards when course selecting. Through hearing their testimonies, our group could understand the main roots of what is working and not working in different platforms.

Creative Research Methods: Web Experiment

We conducted a website experiment using sacrificial concept by creating a website mockup that contains various kinds of realistic and unrealistic, true or fabricated info about a course. The participant was given that he or she is considering taking this course, and he or she could click through various categories to get an idea of the course. We observed which categories, in which order, each participant clicked so that we could learn what his intuitive interests are.

Creative Research Methods: The Card Game

We designed and printed physical cards that allow participants to write down their course names, and sort the cards (courses) according to our questions. Through this activity, we were able to have the participants be fully engaged through thinking with your body and physical interactions.

To synthesize the research that we collected, our team built an affinity map to organize the information and to make sense of the quantitative and qualitative data collected. We concluded that essential information of the course should be kept, emotional aspects from past students should be included, the amount of workload and dropout rate should be more visible, and students should feel comfortable communicating about their course experiences.

Part B /
UI + UX Design: OnionTalk

User Flow

Before diving into the visual design, I used User Flow Mapping to have an overall perspective of the different routes of an individual's experience with OnionTalk. In particular, focusing on what product are we trying to design, to build and to serve the student community.

Mid-Fidelity Wire-frame Sketches

Some process wire-frame sketches before we moved onto implementing the actual UI design using FIGMA.

Login + Course Selection

Through the security of logging in with your school ID and password, (in this case, Andrew ID for CMU students) students do not have to worry about their comments being read by any professors, faculty or staff. They can provide honest feedback and search about any course they want. This enables better accessibility to communication within the student body.

One Click for All External Sources

Based on our research results, the basic information that students wanted to see were the pre-requisites and reservations, external links and syllabi. Providing an accurate and consistent information to every CMU student allows course selecting to be more visible as students can search about any class offered at CMU.

The Discussion Board

OnionTalk is approachable and understanding to students because of its ability to post, read, and search anonymously.

The anonymity in posting honest feedback in the discussion boards is helpful to students because it gives personal experience about the course, professors, workload and difficulty by the students. The discussion board is written by the students for the students.
